People often ask me why a physician would transition into data analytics. The answer isn't simple, but it comes down to this: I wanted to help more people than I could see in a single exam room.

## The Clinical Beginning

I trained as a physician. I learned to diagnose, treat, and care for individual patients. But clinical work has limits. You see one patient at a time. And too often, you see the same problems recurring — not because of individual failure, but because of systemic issues.

## The Pivot

I went to Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health for my MPH because I wanted to understand the systems that shape health outcomes. What I found was that public health organizations were drowning in data but starving for insight.

## Finding the Overlap

My clinical background turned out to be an asset, not a detour. When you've seen how diseases present in real patients, you ask better questions of your data.

## What I Wish I'd Known Earlier

1. **Your clinical knowledge is not wasted.** It gives you context that pure data analysts don't have.
2. **The technical skills are learnable.** STATA, Python, SQL, Power BI — these are tools, not identities.
3. **Communication is the underrated skill.** The best analysis is useless if you can't explain it.

Today, I work at the intersection of clinical insight and population-level data. The best work happens at the intersection.
